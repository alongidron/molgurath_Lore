# Urmeth

**Age:** Unknown    
**Size:** 3.50 m   
**Magic:** ice    
**Source of Magic:** the god of ice Glasivor 
---
# Lore
Urmeth was the Giant King of the North, forged by Glasivor, God of Ice, to stand as both shield and ruler of the frozen lands. From his creation, Urmeth was a warrior of rare virtue. He never ruled from behind a throne—when his people were threatened, he stood at the front lines, blade in hand, bearing the full weight of battle himself.

To sustain him through endless war, Glasivor gifted Urmeth a sacred sword—
a weapon capable of draining the energy of his enemies and channeling it back into its wielder, ensuring that Urmeth would never tire so long as battle continued. With this blade, Urmeth became unstoppable in defense, a living bulwark against all who threatened the North.

He was not only powerful, but kind. Urmeth united the fractured northern tribes and giant clans under a single banner, ending generations of conflict. Together, Urmeth and Glasivor sealed the North against evil, keeping abyssal creatures and demons far from its borders.

Then came the turning point.

Glasivor was forced to leave the North to confront the God of Chaos, entrusting its protection entirely to Urmeth. For the first time, the Giant King stood alone.

The enemy came without mercy.

Hundreds upon hundreds of abyssal creatures and demons poured into the North, attacking with relentless fury. Urmeth and his armies fought with everything they had, but they were outnumbered beyond hope. Seeing his people on the brink of annihilation, Urmeth made a final command—
his armies were ordered to fall back.

Urmeth remained.

In desperation, he unleashed the full power of his blade, no longer limiting what it consumed. The sword drained the energy of everything around him—enemy, battlefield, and even the abyss itself. The invasion was obliterated. The North was saved.

But the victory carried a terrible cost.

By drawing in abyssal power, Urmeth’s mind began to slowly corrupt. His body grew larger, his presence heavier, as if the cold itself bent around him. His eyes burned red, and time no longer seemed to touch him as it once had. Where there had been restraint, there was now dominance. Where there had been mercy, there was fear.

The hero became a tyrant.

Urmeth ruled the North with an iron fist, his sheer power enough to crush any who dared challenge him. None could stand against him—not giants, not demons, not mortals.

When Glasivor finally returned, he was shocked by what Urmeth had become.

Yet… he did not stop him.

So long as Urmeth remained loyal, so long as the North stood protected and chaos was kept at bay, Glasivor allowed the corruption to remain.

From that moment onward, Urmeth was no longer merely a king.

He became the Overlord of the North

Over the years, many Overlords attempted to conquer the North. Each invasion only deepened Urmeth’s hatred of outsiders. One after another, they fell—crushed by his frozen dominion and abyss-scarred power. Urmeth always emerged victorious, and in time his name alone became enough to deter most Overlords from ever testing the North again.

Then Urmeth heard of the Overlords’ Alliance.

He was furious, but not a fool.

Urmeth understood the danger of standing alone against a unified force of rulers like himself. Swallowing his pride, he chose to join the alliance—not out of loyalty, but necessity.

Yet upon entering, one presence unsettled him deeply.

Father Crow.

Urmeth knew little about him. He had heard whispers—of conquest, cruelty, and impossible victories—but what truly disturbed him was the abyssal magic that clung to Father Crow like a living shadow. It reminded Urmeth of the invasion long ago, of the abyssal hordes that had nearly destroyed the North—and of the power that had corrupted him in the first place.

Because of this, and because he trusted outsiders very little, Urmeth rarely appeared at alliance gatherings. Instead, he watched from afar, gathering what information he could about Father Crow.

But information was scarce.

Then, one day, Isyra attempted to visit him.

Urmeth rejected her—again and again—refusing to allow her entry into the North. Only when she declared that she possessed information about the Overlords—and about why Urmeth’s absence mattered—did he finally allow her to pass.

Over time, Isyra became the only Overlord Urmeth truly trusted.

Through her, he began to realize a troubling truth: some members of the alliance were far more powerful than he had believed. And Father Crow… Father Crow was far more dangerous than even the rumors suggested. Urmeth was no longer certain that, should he leave the alliance, he could kill him.

That realization unsettled him.

At Isyra’s urging, Urmeth made a rare decision.

He would appear at the Overlords’ Tournament.

Not to dominate—but to confirm the truth.

There, he witnessed firsthand what Isyra had warned him about. Most of it was true.

Then came the final battle.

Urmeth versus Father Crow.

At first, Urmeth struggled. Father Crow’s speed, technique, and abyss-forged ferocity pushed him back in ways few ever had. But as the battle dragged on, Urmeth invoked the true power of his sword once more.

Energy drained. Strength returned.

With every clash, Urmeth felt Father Crow’s strikes grow lighter, weaker—until the pain nearly vanished entirely. Slowly, relentlessly, Urmeth turned the tide and stood above him, blade raised.

The killing blow was moments away.

Then Charly intervened.

He reminded Urmeth of the law—killing was forbidden.

With a single clap, the stolen energy rushed back into Urmeth’s body all at once. Pain exploded through him as the cost of his power finally caught up.

The arena fell silent.

The Overlords stood in shock—not only because Father Crow had been defeated for the first time in the tournament, but because they had just witnessed the true extent of Urmeth’s power.

From that day on, Urmeth was no longer just feared as the Overlord of the North.

He was feared as one of the few who could defeat Father Crow—and live.

After the battle, Urmeth found himself in poor standing.

At his side stood Isyra, watching him with quiet worry, and Charly, whose anger was unmistakable. Charly made it clear—any future battle between Urmeth and Father Crow was forbidden. The tournament was over. Urmeth was ordered to rest before leaving.

When Isyra asked how he felt, Urmeth surprised even himself.

He felt satisfied.

For the first time, he held genuine respect for Father Crow—not only for his power, but for his control over the abyss. Urmeth no longer viewed him as an enemy. If anything, Father Crow had become a reminder of what discipline over corruption could look like… something Urmeth himself had long since lost.

In the years that followed, Urmeth became slightly more open toward the other Overlords. He still trusted few, but he no longer isolated himself completely.

Then came the call to war.

The Overlords were summoned to invade Hell.

Urmeth opposed the idea immediately. He understood the cost of such a war, the damage it would inflict—not just on realms, but on those who fought it. But the majority of the Overlords agreed, and Urmeth was outvoted.

He had no choice but to join.

In Hell, Urmeth faced some of the strongest Demon Kings to ever exist—and he defeated them all. Victory followed victory. But with each fallen foe, the abyssal corruption within him deepened. His madness grew heavier, louder, harder to contain.

Then it happened.

Urmeth felt Isyra’s death.

Something inside him broke completely.

The last restraint shattered, and Urmeth descended into a blind rampage. Friend and foe became meaningless distinctions. The North’s Overlord unleashed everything—ice, abyss, stolen energy—without control or mercy.

It took multiple Overlords working together to stop him.

In the end, Urmeth was not killed.

He was sealed away.

Thus ended the reign of the Overlord of the North—not in defeat, but in tragedy. A king who had once saved his people, who had stood against chaos itself, was buried beneath seals and silence… feared not for what he had done, but for what he might one day become again.
